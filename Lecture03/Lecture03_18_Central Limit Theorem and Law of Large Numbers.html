Central Limit Theorem and Law of Large Numbers
Law of Large Numbers
Sample mean converges to population mean
\(\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu \text{ as } n \to \infty\)
Convergence in probability
Central Limit Theorem
Sum of random variables approaches normal distribution
\(\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)\)
as \(n \to \infty\)

Real-World Applications
Quality Control
Manufacturing defect rates follow normal distribution with large samples.
\(\bar{X} \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\)
Use: Setting acceptable quality limits in production lines
A/B Testing
Website conversion rates comparison uses CLT for statistical significance.
\(\hat{p} \sim N\left(p, \frac{p(1-p)}{n}\right)\)
Use: Deciding which website design performs better
Opinion Polls
Survey results become normally distributed with sufficient sample size.
\(\text{Margin of Error} = 1.96 \times \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)
Use: Predicting election outcomes with confidence intervals
Financial Risk
Portfolio returns approximate normal distribution over time.
\(R_{\text{portfolio}} \sim N(\mu_p, \sigma_p^2)\)
Use: Value-at-Risk (VaR) calculations for investments
Medical Trials
Average treatment effects tested using normal approximation.
\(t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}\)
Use: Determining if new drugs are effective
Machine Learning
Bootstrap confidence intervals rely on CLT for model evaluation.
\(\text{Accuracy} \sim N\left(\mu_{\text{acc}}, \frac{\sigma^2_{\text{acc}}}{n}\right)\)
Use: Estimating model performance reliability

What is a Galton Board?

The Galton Board, invented by Sir Francis Galton in 1876, is a physical device that demonstrates the Central Limit Theorem. It shows how random binary events combine to form a normal distribution.

1
A ball drops from the top and hits a peg
2
At each peg, the ball goes left or right with probability 0.5 (Bernoulli trial)
3
After passing through \(n\) rows, the final position is the sum of \(n\) random choices
4
Many balls create a binomial distribution ‚Üí approximates normal distribution
Mathematical Connection
\(X = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\), where \(X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(0.5)\)
\(X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, 0.5) \approx N\left(\frac{n}{2}, \frac{n}{4}\right)\)
üí° Key Takeaway
Each ball's path is random, but the collective behavior follows a predictable normal distribution ‚Äî this is the essence of CLT!
